Bootleg Nanite

A GPU driven micropolygon software rasterization shader setup


This is probably one of my favorite technical excursions. I love using unconventional techniques to leverage GPU compute to do wacky stuff.
And the idea of leveraging compute shaders to manually implement custom triangle rasterization for small triangles in order to bypass limitations of the traditional GPU raster pipeline is just so cool.
Obviously I can't take credit for the idea I mean it's just based directly on UE5 nanite stuff. Still it was really fun and interesting to implement it myself for my own purposes.

Here's an initial test just getting polygons on screen from all the chunk data.


And a later test rendering dynamic high detail grass geometry.
This has a few LODs that transition seamlessly due to how detailed everything is. And is also taking advantage of the 64bit intrinsics that make it possible to store depth and visibility data in a single texture using atomic operations.
That is then decoded and rendered with a more normal shader pass. Not really necessary when just rendering with one material, but it's cool so...

AutoDepth

An image viewer to view any image in 3D automatically

The concept of being able to automatically transform images into a 3D experience has always interested me. Really just any means of transforming content into something new with no effort I guess.
Originally I was inspired to make a concept for this once Midas 3 came out and the results seemed like they might finally be decent enough and I had gotten a shower thought on how to render them using ray marching essentially treating the depth map as a distance field.
I think I figured out later that this is basically the same technique used for screenspace reflections in games, tracing the already existing depth and color buffers of the game for relatively cheap reflections.
It took a bit of finagling but with relatively little effort I was able to easily implement this and the results were better than expected. Looking for a simple new project to take on I decided to turn this into an app I could sell.
So I polished it up and made it as simple as possible to run on any hardware without messing with dumb python github projects.
It hasn't gone gangbusters but it's sold alright and reviewed well and I'm glad to have been able to make something that people enjoy.
Probably would have done better but I suck at marketing.
Since then I've occasionally updated it with new models and features.
Available on steam here.

Space Game Prototype

Combat and physical simulation focused space game


I love the idea of space ship combat and stuff but basically every game that exists doesn't really have the vibe I want. They either focus too much on the scale of space battles so much that you lose the personal control and visceral feeling, or they just don't really have the specific flavor of punch I want and use lame weapons like pew pew lasers and energy weapons.
I want more of a vibe of like WW2 battleships and large navy battles, but in space...
Being able to fully customize your weapons and fleet and putting a focus on the physicality of everything being weighty and punchy. Firing a battery of large space guns shooting large kinetic and explosive projectiles should push your ship around y'know.
I also just wanted to have full manual control of everything from the biggest ship to the smallest fighters.
I imagined this game as mostly just a sandbox to mess around in but it never got past the prototype phase because it's hard to say if it really made sense for me to pour my whole life for years into it...
The concepts I made for this were largely inspired by a game called Starsector.

Anyyyway I still ended up making some really cool stuff for it so here it is:
This shows off the manual gun targeting interface and shield stuff, noting the guns have joints and stuff with recoil and camera shake when zoomed in and all that nice stuff.


But the coolest thing I made for this has to be the fully dynamic damage modeling.
This system works based on a volumetric damage map that tracks both deformation and heat.
The result is really cool and allows dynamic deformation across large ship surfaces without any extra modeling effort, it's based on a distance based tessellation which based gives automatic LOD. Ships far away from the camera are basically just surface texture blending, while the closer you get, the more actual deformation you see.
This system was meant to be combined with a more intentional simple fracturing system so that parts of ships can be torn apart or completely blown off.

TP45

FPS GameJam with custom GPU accelerated raytracing, before it was cool

Made quite a long time ago for an obscure GameJam, I pretty much used it as an excuse to try implement a custom GPU accelerated raytracer I'd been working on around that time.
It was interesting because I needed to rework some things so that the acceleration structure could be rebuilt asynchronously to the rendering, because the method I was using could not be updated that fast, nor updated sparsely.
On that note, some elements that needed to be more responsive were rendered traditionally and composited over the top. This was mainly just for the gun and I think particle effects since there wasn't an easy way to get particles into the acceleration structure.
We kept polycounts pretty low but even with this the enemy movement is pretty choppy with the rate that acceleration structure could be updated.

I also had to hack together a system to unload unseen rooms entirely, which ended up causing a bug where you can fall through the floor in hallways, didn't have time to fix that. Sometimes enemies get lost outside of the rooms as well making them unclearable and soft-locking the game.

The only way we could think to take advantage of the raytracing was to add mirrors and allow the laser shots to reflect off of them. It's kind of a gimmick but it is pretty cool shooting at the reflection of an enemy and actually hitting them, and there were score modifiers for doing so.


The laser was actually implemented as a single sample volumetric light, which gives it a cool effect I think.
Pretty simple as all you need to know is whether or not a ray passes through the column of the laser.
 
My partner, Richard did all the models, animation, sound/music.
Overall, kinda mid gameplay wise but it has a vibe.

SYRONO

An original novel project

I think a lot of people had their "Covid-project", and I guess this was mine.
Inspired by a certain game I was playing that ended pretty poorly imo, I just jumped into writing my own story fueled by spite.
I hadn't written much before so it was a novel project for me. (pun intended)
It was pretty difficult at points but I found writing dialogue to be really satisfying and natural.

A novel isn't particularly code related but I still think it definitely belongs as a part of my portfolio.
While writing it I was surprised by how similar writing can be to programming, often the most difficult and the bulk of time spent is problem solving, trying to figure out a series of events to make a certain thing happen. I found this skillset maps well to writing where you have maybe an outline of events you want to happen and certain story beats to hit that creates a problem of how to tie it all together into a cohesive structure, how do your characters get from point A to B.
It's a bit more creative than programming usually is, as in you have a lot more freedom on how to solve that problem, but (if you want a good story) you need it to at least make logical sense.
Less so in figuring out how to make cool and epic moments though...

Overall I'm pretty proud of the result, reading it back is enjoyable so that must mean something, though it's a bit more cringe than it used to be with so much time passed.
Not sure how good it is really, narratively speaking. My friends tell me it's not half bad, but they are also my friends so. To be fair it's also not a genre of story they much care for either. 
At the end of the day though I think maybeee 3-4 people besides myself have actually read it. Turns out it's really difficult to get people to read some random novel if they aren't like your friend who's interested to see what you did with yourself.
But if nothing else this was something cool to see through to the end.


I knew from the beginning that it'd be really cool to have a physical version of something like this, what with being used to only seeing stuff on a screen y'know.
So I think that was a pretty big driving factor for me in getting it done, especially as it was nearing the end and things were getting more and more difficult to figure out how to satisfyingly conclude the story.
But I'm glad I did because-



Another challenging aspect of this was the cover art.
I don't have much traditional art skill, so it was an uphill battle. I'm really glad I pulled it together though because I think the result is pretty cool and makes the whole thing feel that much more real. I think it took somewhere about a month to put the whole thing together, most of which was struggling to draw and shade the characters, basically brute forcing everything.
The background however took like a week to throw together in Unity and was a bit more comfortable for me. Basically just sculpted a simple terrain and slapped a bunch of trees on it and added some simple prefab town in the distance. I modified the shaders used to warp the world to appear more like an inside out planet as that's how it's written in the story, and I think it gives it a nice touch and helps being able to frame everything.
And I was able to make use of my own grass shader setup, GrassFlow, to create the lush fields in the cover art. Especially in the distance it adds a subtle but much needed texture and depth to the terrain.
Creating a nice unique piece of art was also really cool because (somehow) a couple of friends even ended up drawing fanart of my characters, which is insane to see when you're not used to that kind of thing.

3D Fractal

GPU Raymarched Mathematically Generated 3D Fractal Animation

An extension of my experience with ray tracing, I decided it'd be cool to implement a way to ray march as well.
I wont pretend to understand how the math works to generate it, but the concept is a function that generates a signed distance to the surface of the Mandel bulb from a given position, which then lets us ray march the virtual signed distance field to create a cool picture.
Then you can color things by number of marches needed to get nice colors and glow.
To animate it, one of the factors of the function to generate the bulb is just modulated over time.
The end result is this trippy animation.

Cloud Shader

Simple Pseudo-Volumetric Cloud Shader, With Shadows

I whipped this up quickly to test an idea I had.
They're rendered just using some low poly spheres to approximate the volume.
They could look better if I had used better test textures, these are maybe a bit crunchy or wispy, Idunno.



Then they are rendered orthographically into a shadow buffer and projected onto geometry below and treated as shadows.
I think with some combination of lowres and blur to soften them.


Since they're constructed out of multiple spheres, they can cast shadows onto each other as well, which can make for a nice effect.
Scrolling texture layers gives a pretty good flowy effect but it'd be difficult to simulate the way clouds morph over time, though maybe simply scaling/fading them in and out could provide a good enough effect.
Overall though, I think this technique doesn't scale well since you need a ton of them to make a sky feel full and cool, nor would this be able to replicate more complex cloud shapes as easily.
I'm not sure of the performance of this though if you had a ton of them but probably not too bad, the shader is simple so as long as they're properly instanced it might be worth it. Perhaps a procedural spawning system and such could create a nice enough simple sky.
